## What Makes a Dash Cam Good at Night Vision?

This is the most misunderstood aspect of dash cam buying. The marketing term "night vision" covers a huge range of actual capability. Here's what actually determines how well a dash cam performs in the dark:

### 1. Sensor Size and Type

The image sensor is the single most important factor. Larger sensors capture more light per pixel — simple physics.

- **Sony STARVIS / STARVIS 2** — the gold standard in dash cam sensors. Designed specifically for surveillance and low-light video, STARVIS sensors use back-illuminated pixel architecture that captures 2–3x more light than standard sensors of the same resolution.
- **Sony IMX series (IMX307, IMX335, IMX415)** — excellent sensors, often found in mid-range cameras
- **Generic CMOS** — adequate for daytime, struggles at night

The STARVIS 2 (second generation) improves on the original STARVIS with better colour accuracy at low light and less noise in shadow areas.

### 2. Aperture (f-number)

A lower f-number means a wider lens opening, which means more light reaches the sensor. Common dash cam apertures:

- f/1.6 — excellent (more light)
- f/1.8 — very good
- f/2.0 — good
- f/2.8 — adequate for well-lit streets

The Viofo A229 Plus has an f/1.6 aperture — one of the widest in its class.

### 3. HDR and WDR Processing

High Dynamic Range (HDR) / Wide Dynamic Range (WDR) is the camera's ability to handle extreme contrast — like a bright oncoming headlight alongside a dark road surface. Without HDR, either the headlights blow out (overexposed) or the road is invisible (underexposed). Good HDR processing keeps both visible.

### 4. Resolution

Higher resolution means more pixels, which helps in low light — a 2K frame has more data to work with than a 1080p frame when details like number plates are being extracted.

## Night Vision Performance in Indian Conditions

Indian roads present a specific set of night vision challenges:

**Mixed lighting:** Street lights are inconsistent — brilliant sodium-vapour lamps followed by 200m of complete darkness. A good night vision camera must handle both extremes, sometimes in the same clip.

**Oncoming headlights:** Indian roads frequently have vehicles driving with high beams on, even in the middle of the city. A camera with poor HDR will produce a white blur with no detail around oncoming vehicles.

**Number plate capture:** India uses a mix of high-intensity reflective plates, faded plates, and after-market plates. A budget camera may capture the plate area but the text is illegible. STARVIS 2 cameras capture text on most reflective plates even in partial darkness.

**Weather:** Monsoon night driving — rain, fog, spray from other vehicles — significantly degrades even premium cameras. In heavy rain, treat any footage as supplementary rather than definitive.
