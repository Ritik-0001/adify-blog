## Mi 3i vs Cheaper Alternatives — Is BIS and Capacity Honesty Worth It?

This is where budget shoppers get burned. You will find dozens of power banks on Amazon and Flipkart priced at ₹400–₹600 claiming 10,000mAh or even 20,000mAh. Here is why those are a bad idea:

**Capacity dishonesty:** Many no-brand units use recycled cells with 3,000–5,000mAh of actual usable energy despite the label. You will notice your phone barely charges once when the power bank claims two full charges.

**No 18W input:** Cheap units use 5W input. A single full recharge of the power bank takes 7–8 hours overnight, every time.

**No safety protection:** Poorly engineered protection circuits can overcharge your phone battery over months of daily use, shortening phone battery lifespan.

**BIS certification check:** The Mi Power Bank 3i carries BIS certification (IS 16046). Verify this on the Amazon listing under "Technical Specifications" — do not assume.

The ₹300–₹400 premium over no-brand options buys you genuine capacity, real fast input, and actual safety. It is worth it.

## What Charging Speed Does 5V/2.4A Output Mean for Your Phone?

The Mi 3i outputs at 5V/2.4A, which is 12W. This is "standard fast charging" — not Quick Charge, not USB Power Delivery.

For most Android phones, this charges at roughly the same speed as the bundled charger (which is also typically 10W–18W output depending on the model). For iPhones (which cap at 5W on USB-A anyway unless you use USB-C PD), this is perfectly adequate.

If your phone supports Quick Charge 3.0 or Warp Charge and you want those speeds from a power bank, you need to spend ₹1,200–₹1,500+. At the sub-₹1,000 segment, standard output is the norm.
