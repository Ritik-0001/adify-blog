## The Honest Answer: What an Air Fryer Is and Is Not

An air fryer is not magic, and it is not a substitute for every type of cooking. It is a compact convection oven with a powerful fan that circulates hot air rapidly around food to create a crispy exterior without submerging the food in oil.

**What it does well:** Anything you currently deep-fry in oil. Samosas, pakoras, fries, chicken tikka, paneer tikka, bread rolls, mathri, nuggets, fish — anything that relies on a crispy exterior from oil contact can be replicated in an air fryer with 1–2 tablespoons of oil instead of 500ml–1L.

**What it does not do:** Dishes that require the food to be submerged in oil for textural reasons — traditional gulab jamun, jalebi, some types of poori. These require oil submersion and cannot be replicated in an air fryer. (Poori can be air-fried but the texture is closer to baked bread than traditional poori.)

## Indian Food Air Fryer Test Results

Here is an honest assessment of how common Indian fried dishes perform in an air fryer:

**Samosas (frozen or homemade):**
Air fryer result: Excellent. The pastry shell crisps to a golden brown identical to deep-fried. The filling heats thoroughly. At 180°C for 12 minutes, you cannot tell the difference from deep-fried samosas at a glance or by taste. Oil used: 1 teaspoon spray vs 500ml for deep-frying.

**Pakoras (onion/pyaaz, mirchi, mixed vegetable):**
Air fryer result: Very good, with a caveat. Air-fried pakoras are slightly drier and less fluffy than deep-fried ones — the oil in traditional pakoras contributes moisture to the interior. Spraying oil on the pakoras before air-frying helps. At 180°C for 12–14 minutes, the exterior is crispy and the interior is cooked. Most people cannot distinguish them in a blind taste test. Some purists will notice the difference.

**Chicken tikka (marinated bone-in or boneless):**
Air fryer result: Excellent. Marinated chicken air-fries beautifully at 200°C for 20–22 minutes. The yogurt and spice marinade caramelises on the outside for char-like browning. The inside stays juicy because air fryer cooking is faster than oven roasting. This is one of the best air fryer dishes for Indian cooking.

**French fries:**
Air fryer result: Very good. Thin-cut fries at 180°C for 15 minutes with 1 tablespoon of oil are crispy outside, fluffy inside. Thick-cut wedges need 20–22 minutes. The result is lighter than deep-fried chips — some people prefer this, some miss the oil flavour.

**Bread rolls (stuffed Indian bread rolls):**
Air fryer result: Excellent. Crispy exterior, hot stuffed interior. At 180°C for 10–12 minutes, indistinguishable from deep-fried in texture.

**Mathri:**
Air fryer result: Good. The thin-fried snack crisps well at 180°C for 12–14 minutes. You do not need to add any oil since mathri dough already contains ghee or oil.

**Aloo tikki:**
Air fryer result: Very good. At 190°C for 12–15 minutes (flipping once at 8 minutes), aloo tikki gets a good crust. Brush or spray with oil before cooking for best results.

## Oil Savings Calculation: The Real Health Benefit

Deep-frying samosas for a family of 4 uses approximately 500ml of oil per session (you need oil depth for submersion). Not all of it is consumed — oil is reused — but each session degrades the oil and adds to consumption.

Air fryer for the same batch: 1–2 teaspoons of oil sprayed on the samosas. Per cooking session, that is approximately 5–10ml of oil versus 500ml used for deep frying.

Even accounting for oil reuse, a typical Indian family reduces direct oil contact with their fried food by 75–80% with an air fryer. Over a week of cooking fried snacks 3–4 times, that is a meaningful reduction in saturated fat consumption.

## Electricity Cost: Is It Expensive to Run?

This is a common concern. The maths are straightforward.

A typical air fryer (1400W–1700W) running for 20 minutes:

1500W × 0.33 hours = 0.5 units of electricity

At ₹8/unit (the approximate average residential rate in India across states):

0.5 units × ₹8 = ₹4 per 20-minute cooking session

For daily use cooking snacks for 20 minutes, that is ₹4/day or approximately ₹120/month in electricity. Compared to the cost of oil saved, this is negligible.

Compare to a gas burner with kadhai full of oil: gas cost plus oil cost is significantly more per session.

## Who Should NOT Buy an Air Fryer in India

Being honest: an air fryer is not for everyone.

**Do not buy if:**
- You rarely eat fried food — fewer than twice a week on average. The appliance will sit unused.
- Your kitchen has very limited counter space and the footprint (28cm × 28cm for a 4L model) is genuinely a problem.
- You primarily cook wet curries, dals, biryanis — the air fryer adds no value for these dishes.
- You live alone and eat very small portions — a 3.5L basket is almost too big for a single serving; a toaster oven is more versatile.
- You are hoping to reproduce traditional deep-fried textures exactly — air-fried pakoras and pooris are close but not identical to the original.

**You should definitely buy if:**
- Your family eats samosas, pakoras, bhatura, chicken fry, or similar fried snacks 3+ times a week
- You have a family member managing weight, cholesterol, or cardiovascular health who still wants to enjoy fried food occasionally
- You regularly buy fried snacks from street vendors (home air-frying is dramatically cheaper and more hygienic)
- You want to air-fry frozen food (frozen snacks, nuggets, fries) without the mess of deep frying
