## Autofocus: Sony Leads, Canon Is Closing the Gap

Sony's real-time tracking and AI subject recognition (humans, animals, birds, vehicles) has been the industry benchmark for several years. The ZV-E10, A6400, and A7C lines all use the same advanced AF engine — which means even Sony's entry-level cameras offer tracking performance that matches Canon's mid-range offerings.

Canon's Dual Pixel CMOS AF is genuinely excellent — particularly in video mode where it delivers smooth, confident subject acquisition. The EOS M50 Mark II, R50, and R8 all benefit from this system and will satisfy most buyers. However, Sony pulls ahead in complex scenarios: fast-moving sports, wildlife, and multi-subject scenes where continuous tracking matters most.

**Verdict:** Sony wins on autofocus, but Canon's Dual Pixel AF is reliable enough for 90% of Indian buyers.

## Lens Ecosystem in India

**Sony E-mount** is the clear winner in India for lens variety and availability. The E-mount has been around since 2010, giving it a massive catalogue of native lenses, plus adaptor compatibility with almost every legacy mount (Canon EF, Nikon F, Leica M). Third-party options from Sigma, Tamron, and Viltrox are widely available at Indian retailers both online and in stores. The Sony 50mm f/1.8 OSS (under ₹15,000), Tamron 17-28mm f/2.8 (₹40,000), and Viltrox 23mm f/1.4 (₹15,000) are popular with Indian photographers.

**Canon EF-M mount** (used on M50 Mark II) has a limited native lens line — Canon made relatively few EF-M primes and zooms. You can adapt Canon EF lenses with the EF-EOS M adaptor, but it adds bulk and cost. Canon's newer RF mount (used on R50, R8) has excellent native lenses but they are significantly more expensive.

**Verdict:** Sony E-mount wins clearly for lens availability and affordability in India.

## Service Centres in India

Both Sony and Canon have solid authorised service networks across India. Canon has a slight edge in tier-2 and tier-3 cities where its DSLR heritage means more trained technicians. Sony's service centres in metro cities (Mumbai, Delhi, Bengaluru, Chennai, Hyderabad, Kolkata) are well-equipped and efficient. For rural buyers, Canon may be the safer choice purely from a service accessibility standpoint.

## Final Verdict

**Sony wins the mirrorless battle in India for 2026** — better video specs, superior autofocus tracking, and a wider, more affordable lens ecosystem. However, **Canon is the better brand for beginners** who want guided assistance, easier menus, and reliable service everywhere in India. Buy Sony if video content creation is your priority; buy Canon if you're learning photography and want the most supportive ecosystem for the journey.
