## Why Does GPS in a Dash Cam Actually Matter?

GPS in a dash cam does three things that genuinely matter in the Indian context:

**1. Speed logging for insurance claims**

When you're involved in an accident and the other party claims you were speeding, GPS data provides timestamped, frame-by-frame speed records. Your dash cam footage shows you were doing 38 km/h at the moment of impact — not the 80 km/h the other party is claiming. This is not theoretical; Indian insurance disputes routinely hinge on speed, and GPS data is admissible evidence.

**2. Location tagging**

Every frame of GPS-equipped dash cam footage is geo-tagged. You can replay your drive on a map, see exactly where an incident occurred, and provide that location to police or insurance without relying on memory or guesswork.

**3. Time and date stamping (precise)**

GPS syncs your dash cam clock to satellite time — no more footage with wrong timestamps because your camera clock drifted or was never set correctly. Correct timestamps matter legally.

**What GPS data looks like in playback:**

When you play back a GPS-equipped dash cam clip (using the desktop software or app), you see:
- A map with your route and current position
- Your speed at every moment, displayed on the video
- The exact time and date of every frame
- Altitude (less useful, but recorded)

For Indian buyers specifically, the GPS evidence for **toll disputes** is also useful — GPS data proves you were on a particular road at a particular time if there's a billing error.

## Does a Dash Cam GPS Work on Indian Roads?

Yes, reliably. GPS satellites are global — the Viofo and Garmin receivers both work across India, including Ladakh, Northeast India, and rural areas where 4G doesn't exist. GPS is satellite-based and does not depend on mobile networks.

One nuance: **inside parking garages and tunnels**, GPS signal drops. Your dash cam will note the last known position before signal loss and resume logging when you exit.

## External Antenna vs Built-in GPS — Which Is Better?

| | External Antenna (e.g., Viofo A119 Mini 2) | Built-in GPS (e.g., 70mai A500S, Garmin) |
|---|---|---|
| Installation | Wire to route along A-pillar | None — plug and play |
| Signal quality | Often better (antenna can be positioned optimally) | Good but can be affected by metal windshield coatings |
| Aesthetics | Extra wire to manage | Cleaner |

For most buyers, built-in GPS is the more convenient choice. External antenna GPS can perform slightly better in some vehicles with windshield coatings that block GPS signals — but for most Indian cars this is not an issue.
