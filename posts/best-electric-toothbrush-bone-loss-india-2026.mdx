Alveolar bone loss — the destruction of the bone that holds teeth in place — is the terminal consequence of untreated or inadequately managed periodontal disease. If your periodontist or dentist has told you that you have bone loss, this changes how you should choose and use an electric toothbrush significantly.

---

## What Bone Loss Means for Your Brushing Approach

**Critical clinical fact: bone loss is irreversible.** Lost alveolar bone does not regenerate through brushing — even with the best electric toothbrush available. What electric toothbrushes can do for bone loss patients is:

1. **Halt further progression** by removing plaque bacteria from the gumline and interdental areas where the disease-causing biofilm accumulates
2. **Reduce gingival inflammation** that, if chronic, accelerates bone destruction
3. **Allow monitoring** through consistent gum health tracking features in premium models
4. **Protect exposed root surfaces** with the gentlest cleaning action possible — roots are softer than enamel (dentin RDA tolerance is lower) and patients with bone loss often have significant recession exposing root surfaces

**What not to expect:** An electric toothbrush is not a replacement for professional periodontal maintenance (deep scaling, root planing, and 3-monthly recall appointments). It is part of the maintenance protocol — an important part, but not a substitute for professional care.

**Brushing technique for bone loss patients:** Modified Bass technique — placing the bristles at 45 degrees to the gumline — is typically recommended by periodontists. Electric toothbrushes require a modified application of this: hold the brush at the gumline without horizontal scrubbing and let the brush do the mechanical work. Sonic brushes are often preferred by periodontists for bone loss patients because they are gentler.

---

## Must Work With Your Periodontist

Before deciding on an electric toothbrush, discuss with your periodontist:
- Whether you have exposed root surfaces that require extra-soft bristles
- Whether interdental brushes or water flossers are part of your maintenance protocol (they should be)
- The frequency of professional maintenance visits (typically every 3 months for active bone loss patients in India)

The electric toothbrush choice below should be treated as a recommendation to discuss with your periodontist, not an independent decision.

## Indian Context: Bone Loss and Systemic Conditions

Bone loss is disproportionately common in India's diabetic population. India has the world's second-largest diabetic population (approximately 77 million). Diabetes significantly accelerates periodontal bone destruction — the relationship is bidirectional (periodontitis worsens glycaemic control, and poor glycaemic control worsens periodontitis).

Indian patients managing diabetes should:
1. Prioritise periodontal care as part of their diabetes management, not as a separate issue
2. Choose the gentlest effective electric toothbrush (Sonicare Sensitive or Low mode)
3. Maintain 3-monthly professional recall visits regardless of cost — the systemic consequences of uncontrolled periodontal disease in diabetics are severe
