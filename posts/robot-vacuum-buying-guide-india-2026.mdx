## Navigation: LiDAR vs Gyroscope vs Random

Navigation is the single most important specification in a robot vacuum. It determines how efficiently the robot covers your home, how well it avoids obstacles, and whether it can return to clean missed spots.

**Random Navigation (avoid)**
Early robot vacuums used bumper sensors to bounce randomly around the room, eventually covering most of the floor by probability. This works in a completely empty room but fails in furnished Indian homes — the robot gets trapped behind chair legs, misses corners, and cleans some areas multiple times while missing others. Avoid any robot without systematic path planning, regardless of price.

**Gyroscope Navigation (adequate for small homes)**
Gyroscope-based robots use an internal sensor to detect rotation and maintain a straight cleaning path. They clean in rows (much like a human vacuuming) without needing a visual map of the room. Coverage is systematic and complete in simple layouts. These robots cannot avoid obstacles proactively, create reusable floor maps, or handle multi-room routing efficiently. Adequate for straightforward 1 BHK apartments; limiting for 2 BHK and above.

**LiDAR Navigation (recommended)**
LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging) robots spin a laser sensor to build a precise room map before cleaning. They know the exact shape of every room, the location of furniture, and the most efficient cleaning path. They create persistent maps you can edit in an app — defining no-go zones, room boundaries, and cleaning priorities. LiDAR navigation is standard in every robot above ₹18,000 from reputable brands. It is worth the cost step-up from gyroscope models because it handles the dense furniture layouts common in Indian homes effectively.

**Camera + AI Navigation (premium)**
Some models (iRobot Roomba j7+, Dreame L10s Ultra) add cameras to LiDAR, enabling object recognition — the robot identifies shoes, cables, and pet waste and avoids them proactively. Useful for cluttered households but not essential for most buyers.

## Suction Power Explained — Pa Ratings for Indian Floors

Suction power is measured in Pascals (Pa) — the unit of air pressure. Higher Pa means stronger suction. Here is how Pa ranges translate to Indian home conditions:

**Under 2,000 Pa:** Budget robots. Adequate for very light daily dust on smooth tile. Struggles with fine sand, embedded grime, or any carpet. Not recommended as a primary cleaner.

**2,000–3,000 Pa:** Entry-level capable. The Mi Robot Vacuum-Mop 2 Pro at 3,000 Pa is in this range. Handles standard tile and marble floor cleaning in Indian apartments well. Gets dusty fine particles, routine hair, and light debris.

**3,000–4,500 Pa:** Mid-range. The Dreame D9 Max (4,000 Pa) and Xiaomi S10 (4,000 Pa) fall here. Handles Indian summer dust loads, monsoon-tracked grime, and thin area rugs with authority.

**5,000 Pa+:** High-performance. The Roborock S8 (6,000 Pa) and Dreame L10s Ultra (5,300 Pa) in this range handle embedded carpet dirt, high-pile rugs, and pet hair in carpet pile. Overkill for pure tile floors but excellent for mixed-surface Indian homes.

**For most Indian apartments (tile/marble):** 3,000–4,000 Pa is entirely sufficient. You do not need 6,000 Pa unless you have significant carpet coverage.

## Mopping Systems Compared

The mopping capability in robot vacuum-mop combos varies enormously in actual cleaning effectiveness:

**Static Pad Mopping:** A damp cloth drags behind the robot. Provides light moisture on the floor. Effective for very light dust on clean floors, useless for anything grimy. Found in all budget models under ₹20,000.

**Electronic Water Control:** The robot meters water delivery to the pad through a valve, preventing over-wetting. Still a static pad but controlled. The Mi 2 Pro uses this — a meaningful improvement over uncontrolled static pads.

**Sonic Mopping (Roborock VibraRise):** The mop pad vibrates at thousands of cycles per minute, creating a scrubbing action against the floor. Genuinely effective on tile grime and kitchen residue. Found in Roborock models from the S7 series upward. The difference vs static mopping is significant and measurable on Indian kitchen tile.

**Rotating Mop (Ecovacs OZMO Pro):** Spinning mop discs (200 RPM) apply consistent downward pressure while rotating. Effective alternative to sonic mopping. Found in Ecovacs T-series models.

**Self-Washing Dock:** After mopping, the robot returns to its dock where the mop pads are rinsed with water (and in some models, warm water), then dried with hot air. Prevents the mop from redistributing grime on subsequent sessions. Critical for daily mopping in Indian kitchens. Adds ₹20,000–₹40,000 to robot cost but is worth it for habitual daily use.

## Self-Empty Dock — Is It Worth It?

Auto-empty docks collect dustbin contents into a sealed bag in the station, so you only empty the bag every 4–6 weeks rather than emptying the robot's small dustbin every 2–3 days.

**Worth it if:** You have pets (hair volume), a large home (more debris), or simply find manual emptying easy to forget. Also valuable for families with allergies — the auto-empty bag seals allergens rather than exposing them during manual emptying.

**Skip it if:** You have a small 1–2 BHK apartment with modest dust loads, no pets, and are disciplined about maintenance. The add-on cost (typically ₹8,000–₹15,000 for the dock) is not justified for light-use scenarios.

## App Features and Indian Wi-Fi Compatibility

All major brands — Roborock, Dreame, Mi/Xiaomi, Ecovacs, iRobot — have apps that function well in India. A few India-specific notes:

**Wi-Fi Band:** Most robot vacuums require 2.4 GHz Wi-Fi, not 5 GHz. Nearly all Indian home routers provide both bands. Ensure your phone connects to the 2.4 GHz network when setting up the robot for the first time — setup fails if your phone is on 5 GHz during initial pairing.

**Voice Control:** All major models support Alexa and Google Home in India. "Alexa, ask Roborock to start cleaning" works through the Indian Alexa ecosystem. Apple HomeKit support is limited — iRobot has the best HomeKit integration if you use an Apple ecosystem home.

**App Features Worth Checking:** Room-specific cleaning (clean kitchen twice, bedroom once), no-go zones (block off under dining table, playpen area), scheduled cleaning by room, and cleaning history logs. All LiDAR-equipped robots above ₹20,000 from major brands provide these.

## FAQ

**What suction power is enough for Indian homes?**
For tile and marble floors (the majority of Indian homes): 3,000–4,000 Pa is entirely sufficient for daily maintenance. You only need 5,000 Pa+ if you have significant carpet or rug coverage, or if you want maximum pet hair extraction from fabric surfaces.

**How long does a robot vacuum take to clean a 2 BHK apartment?**
A standard 2 BHK of 700–900 sq ft takes 45–75 minutes at normal suction settings with a LiDAR-equipped robot. Budget random-navigation robots may take 90–120 minutes covering the same area less systematically.

**Do robot vacuums work during monsoon season in India?**
Yes, with some management. Increased dust and sand tracked in during and after monsoon season means running the robot more frequently — daily rather than every 2 days. Empty the dustbin more often during peak monsoon months. Keep the robot charging dock away from windows and ensure the robot is dry before docking after cleaning near doorways.

**Can I leave the robot running when I am not home?**
Yes — this is precisely how most users operate these robots. Schedule a morning clean while you are at work. LiDAR robots with room mapping handle unsupervised cleaning well. Ensure loose cables are tucked away and small objects (socks, children's toys) are off the floor before the scheduled run.
