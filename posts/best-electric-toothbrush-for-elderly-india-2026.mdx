## Why Electric Toothbrushes Suit Elderly Users

**Reduced dexterity:** Manual brushing requires the precise back-and-forth wrist motion that becomes difficult with arthritis, Parkinson's disease, or stroke-related hand weakness. Electric brushes require only light contact — the motor does the cleaning motion.

**Consistent cleaning:** Elderly users often develop inconsistent brushing patterns due to fatigue or mobility limitations. Electric brushes with timers and quadrant alerts standardise duration and coverage.

**Gentler on sensitive gums:** Older adults have higher rates of gum recession and sensitivity — sonic brushes' fluid dynamics clean without the friction that can worsen these conditions.

**Denture users:** For partial denture wearers (common in India's 60+ population), electric toothbrushes clean natural remaining teeth more effectively, and water flossers clean under denture clasps.

## Caregiver Setup Guide

**Setting up for an elderly family member:**
1. Charge the brush fully before first use
2. Apply a pea-sized amount of toothpaste to the head (they may apply too much initially)
3. Demonstrate the 45° gumline angle and let the brush do the work
4. Place the charging cradle in a consistently accessible location
5. Label the brush head with tape if multiple household members share a handle

**Recommended toothpaste for elderly:** Sensodyne (sensitivity and gum), Oral-B Gum & Enamel (gum recession protection), or any fluoride toothpaste at 1,000–1,500 ppm. Avoid highly abrasive whitening toothpastes on exposed root surfaces.
