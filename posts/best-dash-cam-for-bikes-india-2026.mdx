## What Makes a Bike Dash Cam Different from a Car Dash Cam?

You cannot simply buy a cheap car dash cam and stick it to your motorcycle. Bikes present a completely different set of challenges:

### 1. Waterproofing Is Non-Negotiable

A car dash cam sits inside a sealed cabin — it never sees rain. A bike dash cam is directly exposed to rain, spray, dust, and humidity. India's monsoon season (June–September) involves sustained heavy rain across most of the country.

The minimum waterproof rating for a bike dash cam used in India: **IPX5** (protected against water jets from any direction). IPX6 or higher is better.

Cameras rated only IP54 or IP55 will usually survive light rain but may fail in heavy monsoon conditions.

### 2. Vibration Resistance

Motorcycle engines vibrate significantly more than car engines, and road vibration on Indian roads (potholes, speed breakers, uneven surfaces) is transmitted directly to anything mounted on the bike. A camera with poor vibration damping will produce shaky, nearly unwatchable footage.

Look for:
- Robust mount design with vibration dampening
- Cameras that have been specifically tested on motorcycles
- Magnetic mounts or lockable brackets that don't work loose over time

### 3. Heat Resistance

A car dash cam is shielded from direct sun by the windshield. A bike-mounted camera sits in direct sunlight, often touching a warm handlebar or mirror. Indian summer temperatures regularly exceed 40°C ambient — camera surface temperatures in direct sun can reach 60–70°C.

Cameras not rated for high ambient temperatures may:
- Shut down due to overheating
- Develop sensor damage over time
- Experience shortened battery/capacitor lifespan

Specifications to look for: operating temperature up to 60°C or higher.

### 4. Mount Security

At 80–100 km/h on a highway, wind forces on a mounted camera are significant. A poorly secured camera can vibrate loose, spin, or detach entirely — which is dangerous.

The Qubo's magnetic mount is a smart solution: the camera snaps onto a magnetised mount base with enough force to stay secure, but can be removed in seconds when you park.

## Mounting Options for Bike Dash Cams

### Handlebar Mount
The most common approach. The camera clamps to the handlebar using a circular mount. Provides a forward-facing view similar to the rider's eye line.

**Pro:** Easy installation, stable mount
**Con:** Camera position is lower than the rider's head, slightly different perspective

### Mirror Mount
Attaches to the mirror stem. Provides a view from higher up, closer to the rider's actual line of sight.

**Pro:** Better perspective, less obstructed view
**Con:** Can vibrate more if mirror is loose

### Helmet Mount
The camera attaches to the helmet, providing a truly first-person perspective.

**Pro:** Exactly what the rider sees
**Con:** Harder to keep the camera charged, cable routing awkward, not all helmet shapes are compatible, not ideal in heavy rain

For legal documentation purposes (accident evidence), a fixed handlebar or mirror mount is more reliable than a helmet mount.
