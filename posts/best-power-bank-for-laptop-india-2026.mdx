## Can Any Power Bank Charge a Laptop? (The USB-C PD Requirement)

Not every power bank can charge a laptop. This is the most important thing to understand before buying.

**Your laptop must have a USB-C charging port.** Laptops that charge via a barrel connector or proprietary magnetic port (older MacBooks with MagSafe 1/2, older ThinkPads, older Dell models) cannot charge via a power bank at all — unless you use an adapter, which is inefficient.

**The power bank must support USB Power Delivery (USB-C PD) output.** Standard USB-A power banks, even at 18W, cannot charge a laptop. You need a power bank with USB-C PD output labeled at 45W, 65W, or 100W.

**Wattage must be sufficient.** Even if both conditions above are met, if the power bank outputs less wattage than the laptop requires, the laptop will charge very slowly (trickle charge) or may drain the power bank while running — it will not power off, but the battery might drain during heavy use.

## 45W vs 65W vs 100W PD — What Do You Actually Need?

**45W PD** is sufficient for: MacBook Air M1/M2 (13"), Dell XPS 13, thin-and-light laptops. Your laptop will charge at full speed or close to it.

**65W PD** covers: MacBook Air M3 (15"), HP Spectre, Asus ZenBook, Lenovo ThinkPad X1, Surface Pro. This wattage covers the vast majority of business and consumer laptops available in India.

**100W PD** is needed for: MacBook Pro 14" and 16", higher-performance gaming laptops with USB-C charging. The Anker PowerCore III 20000 PD tops out at 65W — for 100W, you need units like the Anker 737 Power Bank (higher price).

**Our recommendation:** Buy 65W PD as a baseline if you are unsure. It works for most laptops and many phones. Spending on 45W is fine if you have confirmed your laptop only needs 45W.

## Is It Safe to Use a Power Bank to Charge a Laptop Daily?

Yes — modern USB-C PD power delivery is a standardized protocol. Your laptop negotiates wattage with the power bank safely. There is no risk of damaging your laptop from using a correctly rated PD power bank.

What to avoid:
- Using a non-PD power bank with a USB-C cable hoping it works (it will not charge at meaningful wattage)
- Using very cheap no-brand 65W power banks (PD compliance may be poor, causing unstable wattage delivery)
- Leaving the power bank in high-heat environments while charging (inside a closed car, for example)
